An Urban Exploration Between Habitable Space and Memory

Overview

Al-Qarafa — the City of the Dead — is one of Cairo's oldest inhabited cemeteries, a UNESCO-recognized urban fabric that has sheltered both the dead and the living for centuries. Established during the age of Al-Fustat, it holds layers of Islamic architectural heritage of exceptional historical and spiritual significance.

Over decades of state neglect and aggressive infrastructure expansion, government authorities have demolished cemetery walls, displaced graves, and erased irreplaceable monuments. Simultaneously, economic collapse has pushed thousands of Cairo's poorest residents to occupy the tomb enclosures — building improvised roofs and walls over burial sites, turning them into homes. The result is a city of contradiction: heritage and habitation, memory and necessity, living side by side in a place the state would rather not see.

Intervention — Al-Qasaba

The thesis proposes Al-Qasaba — an urban strategy that refuses the logic of erasure or displacement. Al-Qasaba threads a continuous public route through the necropolis, connecting five nodes of concentrated historic and architectural value: clusters of significant mosques, mausoleums, and monuments, each made accessible and legible through careful restoration and curated public space. The route creates a new form of urban awareness, allowing the city to rediscover a heritage it has been systematically destroying.

The sixth intervention is residential: a co-living typology designed specifically for the current inhabitants of Al-Qarafa. Rather than forcing residents out, the proposal offers an alternative — a dignified way of living that respects the sacred nature of the site and acknowledges the reality of those who have made it their home. The living and the dead continue to share the same urban fabric, but on terms that honour both.
