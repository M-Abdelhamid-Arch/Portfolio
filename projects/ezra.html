Overview

Ezra is a 200+ guest hotel and community centre on an eight-feddan triangular plot in Heliopolis, Cairo — a site shaped by the convergence of two major arterial roads near Cairo International Airport. The constraints of the triangular geometry were not treated as a problem to be resolved but as the formal generator of the project: the building's massing, circulation, and public spaces all respond directly to the acute angles and shifting orientations of the site's three edges.

The project was designed as a group effort — three architects working across a programme that integrates full hotel facilities with civic amenity for the surrounding neighbourhood. A physical monochromatic model was produced alongside the digital drawings, used throughout the design process to test massing decisions and spatial relationships at scale.

Architecture and Context

Heliopolis carries a layered architectural identity: the suburb was founded in the early twentieth century as a cosmopolitan garden city, its streets lined with buildings that fuse European planning principles with Islamic ornamental traditions. Ezra absorbs this duality rather than choosing between its influences. The facade language draws on both — geometric screens, arched openings, and articulated balcony rails read simultaneously as contemporary and historically rooted, belonging to Heliopolis without imitating it.

The ground floor is fully public. Community spaces, an arcade, and a market open onto the street, serving Heliopolis residents independently of the hotel programme above. The hotel volumes are organised vertically, with a planted transition zone separating the civic base from the private guest levels. The aim throughout was civic legibility: a building that a Heliopolis resident can understand and enter, not merely a hotel that occupies their neighbourhood.
