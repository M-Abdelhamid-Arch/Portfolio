Social Housing in Arghilla

Designing Community Where the City Thins Out

Overview

Arghilla is a peripheral neighbourhood of Reggio Calabria with a difficult history — marked by demographic decline, social fragmentation, and a persistent presence of organised crime that has hollowed out its streets and eroded public life. This project takes that condition seriously: housing alone is not the answer. What Arghilla needs is a reason to gather.

The formal strategy draws directly from Jan Gehl's urban theory — particularly his analysis of how building edges, ground-floor activity, and eyes on the street generate safety, belonging, and civic life. The project fills voids in the existing urban fabric, stitching together fragments of the neighbourhood through form rather than imposing a new order over them. The result integrates with what is already there rather than starting over.

Typologies

Three housing types address different residents and life stages. The apartment forms take inspiration from Charles Correa's approach to transparency and privacy: thresholds are graduated rather than abrupt, and units open onto shared courtyards and semi-public terraces where encounter is possible without being forced.

Landscape threads through the entire scheme — communal gardens, covered walkways, and a small civic room anchor each cluster. The ambition is modest and specific: to give a neighbourhood long defined by absence something worth coming outside for.
