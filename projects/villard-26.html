Palermo Rifratta

Villard 26 is part of a larger urban design studio titled Palermo Rifratta — Palermo Refracted — a group investigation into the overlooked infrastructures and forgotten landscapes of Palermo, Sicily. A team of six students each took a different fragment of the city; the work was developed collectively at the urban scale and individually at the architectural scale, with pairs of students working on adjacent or thematically related sites.

The studio's title describes its method: rather than proposing a new vision imposed over the city, each intervention refracts what is already there — amplifying, revealing, and connecting the latent potential within Palermo's existing urban fabric. The city is not remade; it is seen differently.

The Quarry

The site assigned to this pair of interventions is The Quarry — a large disused limestone extraction site embedded within the urban fabric of Palermo. The quarry is not peripheral; it sits within the city, surrounded by dense residential tissue, yet it has been invisible to urban life for decades. Its floors and carved walls record centuries of extraction, and its geology is the same limestone — pietra di Palermo — from which the city itself was built. The quarry is, in a direct material sense, the source of Palermo's urban fabric.

The proposal works with two existing infrastructures on the site: the Cisterne Nervi — a series of underground cisterns whose vaulted concrete structure carries the mark of twentieth-century engineering — and the quarry's carved topography, which offers a natural theatre condition reminiscent of the ancient Greek theatres at Siracusa. Both are treated not as ruins to be preserved behind glass but as active spatial resources, capable of hosting new programmes while remaining fully legible as what they are.

Intervention

The proposal introduces a sequence of public spaces through the quarry floor and into the cisterns, organised along a route that moves from street level down through the excavated landscape to the underground volumes below. The carved walls become terraces and seating for outdoor performance; the cisterns become gallery, event, and gathering space lit by light wells cut through their vaulted roofs. New lightweight structures mark the threshold between city and quarry without competing with the geological drama of the existing terrain.

The reference point for the sculptural approach to the quarry's carved surfaces is the work of Arnaldo Pomodoro — whose bronze spheres, cracked open to reveal intricate interior worlds, offer a formal analogy for the project's central idea: that what is buried and excavated can contain more richness than what sits at the surface. The quarry is not a wound in the city. It is a space the city has not yet learned to inhabit. The project offers a way in.
